Arabic Letters via Hebrew Cognates

A personal reference for learning Arabic script through familiar Semitic roots

ب י ت  ·  بَيْتٌ  ·  בַּיִת
Twenty Arabic words selected for their recognizability through Biblical Hebrew. Each word illustrates one or more new Arabic letters and reflects systematic sound correspondences between the two Semitic sister languages. The highlighted letters in each card are the ones introduced by that word.

Bonus — the missing letter: غ (ghayn)
The one letter without a clean beginner cognate. It is the voiced counterpart of خ, and sometimes corresponds to Hebrew ע. Example: مَغْرِبٌ (maghrib) — west, sunset ↔ Hebrew מַעֲרָב (maʿarāv). You will meet مَغْرِبٌ early in Quranic reading.

Key Sound Correspondences

Hebrew Arabic Rule Example pair
שׁ ش שׁ → ش (when pronounced sh) שֶׁמֶשׁ → شَمْسٌ
שׂ / שׁ س Hebrew collapsed שׂ and שׁ; Arabic preserves the s sound separately שָׁלוֹם → سَلَامٌ
ז ذ Voiced dental — Heb. z ↔ Ar. dh זָהָב → ذَهَبٌ
ט ط Emphatic t — clean direct match טוֹב → طَيِّبٌ
צ ص / ض / ظ Hebrew צ corresponds to three Arabic emphatics. Arabic preserved the distinctions Hebrew merged. צַדִּיק → صَادِقٌ
אֶרֶץ → أَرْضٌ
צֵל → ظِلٌّ
ע ع / غ Arabic split ע into two letters עַיִן → عَيْنٌ
מַעֲרָב → مَغْرِبٌ
כ (fricative) خ Velar fricative — like German ch in Bach חוֹתָם → خَاتَمٌ

Suggested Study Order

Week 1 — Near-identical: بَيْتٌ · يَوْمٌ · عَيْنٌ · مَلِكٌ · نَبِيٌّ · سَلَامٌ

Week 2 — Sound-shift words: شَمْسٌ · ذَهَبٌ · كِتَابٌ · زَيْتٌ · فَمٌ · حَيَاةٌ

Week 3 — The emphatics cluster: أَرْضٌ · ظِلٌّ · صَادِقٌ · طَيِّبٌ · قُدُّوسٌ

Week 4 — Remaining & review: جَبَلٌ · ثَلَاثَةٌ · خَاتَمٌ · مَغْرِبٌ (غ bonus)

Tashkīl — The Diacritical Marks (تَشْكِيل)

Tashkīl marks are the small signs written above or below Arabic letters to indicate short vowels, doubled consonants, and the absence of a vowel. The Quran is always printed fully vowelled — so reading with tashkīl is the norm, not the exception. These marks have rough parallels in Hebrew niqqudot.
Mark Name Meaning Example Hebrew parallel
بَ فَتْحَة (fatḥa) Short a — a small diagonal stroke above the letter بَيْتٌ bayt פַּתַח — same name, same sound, same position above the letter
بِ كَسْرَة (kasra) Short i — a small stroke below the letter كِتَابٌ kitāb חִירִיק — same short i sound; Hebrew places it below as well
بُ ضَمَّة (ḍamma) Short u — a small waw-shaped curl above the letter قُدُّوسٌ quddūs קִבּוּץ / שׁוּרֵק — Hebrew's short and long u vowels
بْ سُكُون (sukūn) No vowel — a small circle above the letter, indicating a "closed" consonant with no following vowel يَوْمٌ yawm — the و carries sukūn שְׁוָא נָח (silent shewa) — marks a consonant with no vowel or a reduced one
بّ شَدَّة (shadda) Doubled consonant — a small ش-shaped sign above the letter. The consonant is held twice as long. نَبِيٌّ nabiyy — the ي is doubled דָּגֵשׁ חָזָק — dagesh forte doubles the consonant in exactly the same way
بً تَنْوِين فَتْح (tanwīn fatḥ) -an ending — double fatḥa; marks indefinite accusative or common noun endings in citation form بَيْتًا baytan No direct parallel; Hebrew indefiniteness is unmarked, but the -n echo is a Semitic feature seen in some archaic Hebrew forms
بٍ تَنْوِين كَسْر (tanwīn kasr) -in ending — double kasra; indefinite genitive مَلِكٍ malikin The three tanwīn forms together mark grammatical case on indefinite nouns — Arabic kept the case system Hebrew lost.
بٌ تَنْوِين ضَمّ (tanwīn ḍamm) -un ending — double ḍamma; indefinite nominative. This is the citation form used in dictionaries. مَلِكٌ malikun
آ مَدَّة (madda) Long ā over alif — a small tilde-like stroke (~) written over ا when it follows a hamza. Represents a lengthened a. قُرْآنٌ Qurʾān קָמָץ / צֵרֵי — long vowels marked above; the lengthening principle is the same
أ / إ / ئ / ؤ هَمْزَة (hamza) The glottal stop — same sound as Hebrew אָ. Written above alif, below alif, above waw, or above ya (without dots), depending on surrounding vowels. أَرْضٌ arḍ אָלֶף — same glottal stop consonant. Arabic makes its position explicit with the hamza sign; Hebrew uses alef as a silent letter or vowel seat.
The tanwīn in the word cards above: All 20 words are given in their dictionary citation form, which ends in ـٌ (tanwīn ḍamm = -un). This is the indefinite nominative — the form you will see in glossaries. When you read the Quran in context, the ending will vary or drop entirely. Don't let it confuse you — the root and the core consonants are what matter.
